Transfer of Training — Evaluation Framework

The Bridge

Training is only the beginning. What happens at the bedside — in the days and weeks after — is the measure that matters.

Why Most Training Fails

The $190 Billion Problem

Organizations spend $190 billion annually on workplace learning. Research suggests only about 10% of that training ever transfers into actual changed behavior on the job. The rest evaporates — not because the training was bad, but because no one built the bridge between the training room and the workplace.

This program was designed against that failure from the beginning. Every element of this program exists not to transfer information, but to change the way a clinician stands at a patient's bedside on discharge day. This page is how we know whether it worked.

The Science Behind This Program

Seven Factors. All of Them Matter.

Research from 498 interviews across 17 professional groups — judges, teachers, physicians, firefighters — identified seven intertwined factors that determine whether learning transfers. This program was built to address every one of them.

1
Planning Process
A representative team — planners, trainers, content experts, and learners — shapes the program together.
↳ This program was built by a patient. That is the planning process.
2
Learner Characteristics & Motivation
Transfer requires internal motivation — self-efficacy, confidence, and genuine desire to apply learning.
↳ The pre-work module and Serge's story activate this before session one begins.
3
Design & Delivery Methods
Content must be designed for application, not passive reception. Show-me, not tell-me.
↳ Every activity in this program requires demonstration, not comprehension.
4
Learning Context
The environment of the training must mirror the environment of application.
↳ The Transition Circle and Pop-Up Rules reshape the room to match the bedside.
5
Immediate Application
Transfer probability drops sharply when application is delayed. Practice must begin the same day.
↳ The show-me competency check happens before any participant leaves the room.
6
Workplace Environment
Transfer requires supervisory support and a culture that reinforces new behaviors after training ends.
↳ The two peer debrief sessions keep the culture alive after the training room closes.
7
Eliminating Barriers
Systemic obstacles — unclear expectations, no follow-up, reverting to old norms — must be actively removed. Transfer is a team sport. It involves learners, instructors, supervisors, and in this context, the patients themselves.
↳ The three-touch evaluation arc below is how this program eliminates the most common barrier: invisibility after training ends.
The Evaluation Arc

Three Touches. One Truth.

Most programs ask one question at the end of one session. This program asks across time — because transfer is not an event. It is a process. The three-touch arc below tracks that process from the training room to the bedside and back.

1
End of Session — Day One
Immediate Response
Every participant completes Form 1 anonymously before leaving the room. This captures first impressions, emotional response, and stated intentions. It is not a test. It is a baseline — the before photo. The form is below.
2
Two Weeks After Session
The Peer Debrief — No Instructors
The group reconvenes without the training team. No authority. No evaluation. Just peers telling each other the truth about what happened when they went back to the bedside. A single structured prompt guides the conversation. The prompt is below.
3
Four Weeks After Session
Full Group Return — Did It Cross the Bridge?
The entire group — care team and training leads — reconvenes to measure actual transfer. What changed at the bedside? What didn't? What needs to be built into the next session? Form 2 captures this. The same three questions as Form 1. The growth becomes visible. Form 2 is below.
Touch One — End of Session

Form 1: The Baseline

Complete this form before you leave today. There are no right answers. Your response is anonymous. What you write here will be compared — only by you — to what you write in four weeks.

Evaluation Form — Session One 🔒 Anonymous · Baseline
Question 01 · Multiple Choice

Choose the one that is most honest, not most ideal.

Touch Two — Two Weeks After Session

The Peer Debrief

The training team is not in this room. That is intentional. What gets said in a room without authority is the closest thing to the truth available. This session belongs to the care team.

One prompt. One hour. No agenda beyond honesty.

The Prompt
"Tell the group about one moment since training where you almost defaulted to the old way — and what you did instead."

If you did default to the old way — tell that story too. That is equally valuable. More, even.

  • No titles in this room. The rules from session one still apply.
  • One voice at a time. Everyone speaks before anyone speaks twice.
  • What is said in this room stays in this room — unless the group decides otherwise.
  • If the old system made it impossible to do it the new way — name the system. Not the person.
  • End by identifying one thing the group wants to bring back to the full session in two weeks.
Touch Three — Four Weeks After Session

Form 2: Did It Cross?

The same three questions. Four weeks later. The distance between your answers now and your answers then — that distance is the measure of this program. Complete this before the full group debrief begins.

Evaluation Form — Four Week Follow-Up 🔒 Anonymous · Transfer Measure
Question 01 · Multiple Choice
Since the training session, which of the following best describes your discharge practice?
Choose the one that is most honest. Growth shows in the gap between this answer and your answer four weeks ago.
Question 02 · Short Answer
Describe one specific thing you did differently at the bedside in the past four weeks as a result of this training. If nothing changed — describe what got in the way.
Both answers are valuable. The barrier named is the barrier that can be fixed.
