Measuring Impact

A program that cannot prove it worked is a program that cannot be trusted. This is how we prove it.

Why Measurement Is Not Optional

Good intentions are not evidence.

Every training program believes it is making a difference. Belief is not enough. The Transplant Discharge Wellness Group was built because a real patient went home alone in a cab with paperwork no one walked him through. The only way to know whether this program prevents that from happening again is to measure — rigorously, repeatedly, and honestly — whether the care team's behavior at the bedside actually changed.

This page is the evaluation infrastructure that makes that measurement possible. It is built on Kirkpatrick's Four-Level Model and expanded into a 30-day Evidence Portfolio that gives the department head a clear, documented answer to the question that actually matters: did the training cross the bridge?

Artifact One — The Evaluation Portfolio

The Kirkpatrick Framework — Applied

Kirkpatrick's Four-Level Model Donald Kirkpatrick, 1959 — The standard for training evaluation in professional practice.
Each level asks a harder question than the one before it. Level 1 is easy to answer. Level 4 is the one that matters. Most programs stop at Level 1 and call it evaluation. This program does not stop until Level 4 — because the patient at the discharge desk cannot afford anything less.

"Did the training feel relevant, respectful of my experience, and worth my time?"
Captured via Form 1 (end of session) — the anonymous baseline evaluation already embedded in The Bridge. Measures perceived value, emotional response, and stated intention to apply. This is the floor, not the ceiling.

Level 4 — What We Measure & How

Pre-Training Baseline vs. Post-Training Actuals

These four metrics are pulled from institutional data before the first training session begins, establishing a baseline. The same metrics are pulled at 30, 60, and 90 days post-training. The delta is the evidence.

30-Day Transplant Readmission Rate
The most direct measure of post-discharge failure. A readmission within 30 days is often a discharge problem in clinical disguise.
Pre-Training
Baseline %
Pulled from last 12 months of unit data before training begins.
Post-Training Target
↓ 15–20%
Measured at 30, 60, and 90 days. Trend matters as much as number.
Signal Identification Rate at Discharge
What percentage of patients leave with a named, documented Signal. This is a process metric — directly tied to training content.
Pre-Training
Baseline %
Estimated near 0% — this role did not formally exist before training.
Post-Training Target
↑ 100%
Non-negotiable. Every patient. Every discharge. No exceptions.
Documented Discharge Gap Incidents
Incidents formally documented where a gap in discharge readiness was identified and escalated — rather than silently overlooked.
Pre-Training
Baseline #
Low documentation does not mean few gaps. It means few were named.
Post-Training Target
↑ Visibility
An increase in documented gaps initially indicates improved awareness — not worsened care.
Medication Adherence Flag Rate
Flags raised at first post-discharge follow-up appointment for medication non-adherence, including access barriers and supplement interactions.
Pre-Training
Baseline %
Pulled from transplant clinic follow-up notes, prior 12 months.
Post-Training Target
↓ Flags
Reduction indicates issues are being caught and resolved before discharge, not after.

Artifact Two — The Evidence Portfolio

How Learners Demonstrate Skills 30 Days Later

"A skill that cannot be demonstrated is a skill that has not been learned. This portfolio is not paperwork. It is the care team's proof — to themselves, to their peers, and to their department head — that what happened in the training room crossed the bridge to the bedside."

01
Work Sample
The Discharge Readiness Record
Submitted at Week 4
What It Is
A de-identified summary of one actual discharge the learner managed in the 30 days following training. The learner documents what they did differently — what they checked that they would not have checked before, what gap they caught, what conversation they had that they would have previously avoided. Patient identifying information is removed. Clinical details remain.
Evaluation Criteria
  • Signal identified and documented before discharge
  • At least one medication access or interaction issue assessed
  • 72-hour plan discussed with patient and caregiver
  • One show-me demonstration completed and noted
  • Any gap identified — and what action was taken
02
Self-Assessment
The 30-Day Reflection
Submitted at Week 4
What It Is
A structured written reflection — three questions, answered honestly. Not evaluated for the "right" answers. Evaluated for specificity and honesty. A reflection that says "I did everything better" tells the department head nothing. A reflection that names one thing that remained hard and one barrier the system needs to address tells them everything.
The Three Questions
  • What is one specific thing you did differently at the bedside in the last 30 days?
  • What is one thing from training you tried to apply and could not — and what got in the way?
  • What does this unit need to do differently at the systems level to make the training stick?
03
Peer Witness Statement
The Colleague Confirmation
Submitted at Week 4
What It Is
A brief written statement from one colleague who worked alongside the learner during a discharge in the 30 days following training. The witness does not evaluate performance. They describe what they observed. One paragraph. One specific moment. The colleague's name is on the statement — accountability is mutual, and intentional.
What the Witness Describes
  • One specific behavior they observed during a discharge
  • Whether it was different from what they typically see
  • Whether it appeared to affect the patient or caregiver's readiness
  • One sentence on what they themselves will do differently as a result of watching

The verdict the department head is looking for: A meaningful shift in Form 1 to Form 2 Likert scores, combined with a Signal identification rate approaching 100% and a directional decline in 30-day readmissions, is sufficient evidence at 30 days to continue the program as designed. The 90-day pull will confirm whether the trend holds. If readmissions are unchanged but Signal rates are high, the program is working — the system around it needs to catch up.
