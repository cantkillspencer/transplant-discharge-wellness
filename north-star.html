Learning Objectives & Competencies

The North Star

What this program is designed to produce — stated plainly, measured honestly, and translated into the language of the people doing the work.

Why Objectives Matter

A promise written in two languages.

Learning objectives exist for two audiences who rarely speak to each other. Institutions need measurable, taxonomic language — verbs like "analyze" and "construct" that can be assessed and documented. Clinicians need human language — sentences that answer the question every adult learner asks before they commit to anything: what is in this for me?

This page holds both. The Bloom's Taxonomy Map is the program's promise to the institution. The WIIFM Dashboard is the program's promise to the person in the chair. They are the same promise. Just spoken in different rooms.

Artifact One — Bloom's Taxonomy Map

Five Objectives. One Direction.

These five objectives are sequenced from higher-order to foundational thinking — each one corresponding to a specific competency gap this program was built to close. They are measurable, observable, and tied to real performance at the bedside. The first objective is the anchor. Everything else is built around it.
Evaluate
Bloom's Level 5
Evaluate the family system of each transplant patient — including relational history, support capacity, and potential barriers to post-discharge care — and use that assessment to develop an individualized discharge support plan before the patient leaves the unit.
★ Anchor Objective
↳ Addresses the gap between logistics-based discharge and human-centered discharge
Construct
Bloom's Level 6
Construct a personalized "Day One through Week Eight" recovery framework for each patient that maps what a healthy day looks like at each stage — so the patient and their support system can recognize when something is wrong before it becomes a crisis.
↳ Addresses the 72-Hour Planning gap and extends it through the full recovery arc
Analyze
Bloom's Level 4
Analyze each patient's complete medication profile — including timing, interactions with current supplements, and potential access barriers (financial, logistical, or social) — and develop a contingency plan for medication continuity before discharge is signed.
↳ Addresses the Standardized Validation gap and the Ownership gap
Design
Bloom's Level 6
Design a structured check-in plan with each patient's designated Signal — their named post-discharge accountability partner — that establishes contact cadence, escalation protocol, and a clear pathway back to the care team if anything changes.
↳ Addresses the Ownership gap and the absence of structured post-discharge accountability
Apply
Bloom's Level 3
Apply self-monitoring practices that allow care team members to recognize when they are approaching capacity, communicate that clearly to their team, and access support before it affects patient care.
↳ Addresses caregiver sustainability — the condition under which all other objectives remain possible
Artifact Two — The WIIFM Dashboard

What This Actually Means For You

"Adults do not learn in order to know. They learn in order to do. Every objective above was written for an institution. Everything below was written for you — the person who has to go back to that unit on Monday and make these things real."

— Adapted from Malcolm Knowles, The Modern Practice of Adult Education

🧭
Anchor Objective · Evaluate
Understanding the Family — Not Just the Name on the Form Anchor
Academic language: "Evaluate the family system of each transplant patient, including relational history, support capacity, and potential barriers to post-discharge care."
What this means for you: You will never again hand a patient a discharge bag without knowing who — or what — is waiting for them at home. Not just a name and a phone number. The real picture. Because a transplant doesn't happen in a vacuum. There is always a story. Genetics. History. Distance. Estrangement. Love. Fear. You deserve to know that story before you sign. And so does your patient.
This objective will give you a structured, non-invasive way to have the conversation that everyone on the care team has been quietly avoiding — and the language to document what you find so that the next person who touches this patient doesn't have to start from zero. It is the objective that changes everything else.
📅
Objective 2 · Construct
A Map for the New Life
Academic language: "Construct a personalized Day One through Week Eight recovery framework."
What this means for you: Your patient will leave knowing what a good day looks like — and what a bad day looks like. That means fewer panic calls at 2am from a caregiver who doesn't know if what they're seeing is normal. It means your patient has a mental framework to say "this isn't how they said it should go" — and knows to call before it becomes an emergency. You give them a map. They stop driving blind.
💊
Objective 3 · Analyze
Medications That Actually Work in Real Life
Academic language: "Analyze each patient's complete medication profile including timing, interactions, and access barriers."
What this means for you: You will know — before they leave — whether your patient can actually get and take their medications. Not just whether they understand the dosage. Whether they can afford it next month. Whether their supplement routine will interfere with it. Whether there's a person who can pick it up if they can't. The medication that doesn't get taken is the medication that wasn't really prescribed.
📡
Objective 4 · Design
Every Patient Has a Signal
Academic language: "Design a structured check-in plan with each patient's designated Signal."
What this means for you: No patient leaves without a named person who is accountable for knowing how they are doing. Not "a family member." A specific human being with a specific commitment and a specific way to reach the care team. You will know who that person is, how to reach them, and what they've agreed to. That is not paperwork. That is a lifeline.
🫁
Objective 5 · Apply
Taking Care of the Person Taking Care
Academic language: "Apply self-monitoring practices to recognize and communicate capacity limits."
What this means for you: You will have the language and the permission to say "I need support" before you're too depleted to say it. Transplant discharge is the hardest moment in the continuum of care. You are being asked to hold the most complex, most emotionally loaded transition in a patient's life. This objective exists because you cannot pour from an empty vessel — and because the team around you needs to know when you're running low.
Introducing — Objective 4 in Practice

Meet The Signal

Every patient needs one person who is paying attention after the team goes home. Not a hotline. Not a pamphlet. A human being with a name, a number, and a commitment. We call this person The Signal — because their job is simple and non-negotiable: to send one when something isn't right.

The Signal
"The person who keeps the line open."
Not a caregiver in the clinical sense. Not a case manager. A designated, named human being — chosen before discharge — whose role is to stay in contact, pay attention, and know exactly how to reach the care team if anything changes. Before the patient leaves this unit, their Signal is identified, briefed, and on record.
→
Named before discharge. The Signal is identified and documented as part of the discharge process — not assumed or left to chance.
→
Briefed, not burdened. The Signal knows what to watch for, who to call, and that asking for help is the right thing to do — not a failure.
→
On the record. The Signal's name and contact information is part of the patient's discharge documentation — visible to the full care team.
→
If there is no Signal, discharge is not complete. Finding or building one is part of the care team's responsibility — not the patient's.
"The objective of education is not to fill a bucket.
It is to light a fire — and then make sure there is someone there to tend it."
— Transplant Discharge Wellness Group, Learning Design Principles
