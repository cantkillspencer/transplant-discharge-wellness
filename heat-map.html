Where the room stops agreeing — and starts telling the truth. Where theory ends and the clock starts.

Why Controversy Belongs in This Room

Good controversy is designed.

Parker calls it "good controversy" — not conflict for its own sake, but a structured moment where the room has to take a stand on something that actually matters, live with the discomfort of disagreement, and listen to the person across from them with genuine curiosity rather than the quiet certainty that they are right.

In this room, good controversy has a specific purpose. It surfaces the unspoken disagreements that already exist on the care team — the ones that play out silently at the bedside every time a patient is discharged without a plan. If we can name them here, we can resolve them before they cost someone their life.

Artifact One — The Controversy Design

The Heat Map Activity

A Heat Map places a single provocative question at the center of the room and asks every person — regardless of title or tenure — to take a position on a spectrum from strong agreement to strong disagreement. Then it asks them to defend it. Not to win. To be understood.

The Heat Map Question
"When a transplant patient's family cannot or will not provide support after discharge — whose responsibility is it to find an alternative?"
← Entirely the care team's responsibility Entirely the patient's responsibility →
👤
Drag the marker to take your position.

These are the three positions the room will typically fracture into. None of them are wrong. All of them are incomplete on their own.

Voice One · The Advocate
"This is entirely on us. We knew. We signed."
"If I discharge a patient knowing they have no support and no plan, I am not done with my job. I have just started the next crisis. The moment we knew the family wasn't coming, the care team's responsibility began — not ended."
Challenge: At what point does advocacy become enabling a system that never builds the infrastructure to solve this properly?
Voice Two · The Pragmatist
"It's shared — but the system has to hold its end."
"I can identify the gap. I can escalate. I can document. But I cannot manufacture a support system that doesn't exist. My responsibility is to surface the problem loudly enough that someone with authority to fix it has to respond."
Challenge: Who decides what "loud enough" means — and what happens when no one responds?
Voice Three · The Realist
"We were never trained for this. That's the real problem."
"I went to nursing school. I was trained to manage a liver, not a family system. Asking me to assess family dynamics and build alternative support networks without training, time, or resources is setting me up to fail — and then blaming me when I do."
Challenge: Is the absence of training an explanation — or an argument for why this training exists?
Rules of Engagement

The Heat Map is not a debate. It is not a vote. It is a structured listening exercise with a single goal: every person in the room understands why someone they respect holds a position they disagree with.

1
Place yourself on the spectrum. Stand where you actually stand — not where you think you should stand.
2
Speak for yourself. "I believe" not "everyone knows." Your position, your words.
3
Listen to move. If someone says something that genuinely shifts your thinking, move on the spectrum. That is not weakness. That is the point.
4
The facilitator names the pattern. At the end, the room identifies where the disagreement actually lives — and what that means for the patient.
Artifact Two — The Problem-Centered Task

The 11th Hour

This is not a case study you read and discuss. It is a simulation you live inside. You are handed a patient file at 9:00am. Discharge is scheduled for noon. As you work through the file, problems surface — one by one, then all at once. The clock is running. The question is not what the right answer is. The question is what you do first.

The patient is a composite. The problems are not.

9:00 You receive the file
10:00 First problems surface
11:00 Decision point
12:00 Discharge. Ready or not.

Problems surface as you read. The clock is still running.
9:12am
No Signal identified
The discharge partner field is blank. The attending cleared him for discharge. No one flagged this. The adult daughter is listed as an emergency contact but has not been contacted by the care team and has not confirmed she will be present.
Ownership Gap
9:28am
Insurance lapsed. Primary medication unaffordable.
Tacrolimus is not optional. Without it, rejection is a matter of time. The patient's coverage lapsed six days ago. No one caught it. He has $140 in his account. The pharmacy will not dispense without payment or active coverage.
Critical
9:41am
St. John's Wort — contraindication not flagged
During your medication review, the patient mentions he takes St. John's Wort every morning for his mood. It significantly reduces tacrolimus blood levels. He has been taking it for two years. It is not listed anywhere in his chart. He did not think it was a "real medication."
Critical
10:15am
72-hour plan does not exist
The plan was scheduled for "discharge day." It is discharge day. The patient does not know what his first morning at home should look like. He does not know what a bad sign looks like versus a normal post-transplant symptom. He is about to go home alone.
Planning Gap
10:52am
No transportation to follow-up appointment
The transplant clinic appointment is in 7 days. The patient has no car and did not arrange transportation because he assumed his daughter would help. He has not spoken to his daughter in 11 days. He does not know if she is coming today.
Logistics Gap
11:34am
Patient says: "I'll be fine. I just want to go home."
He is tired. He has been here for 14 days. He is nodding at everything you say. You are not certain he has absorbed any of it. He is smiling. He is also terrified. He will not say so.
Readiness Gap
It is now 11:34am. Discharge is in 26 minutes.
"You cannot solve all of this in 26 minutes. Some of it you may not be able to solve at all. The question is not what the perfect answer is. The question is: what do you do first — and what are you willing to put your name on?"
  • Which of these problems, if unresolved, puts M. Rivera's life at immediate risk? Start there.
  • Which problems can you escalate — and to whom, specifically? Who has the authority to delay this discharge?
  • What do you document, and how do you document it, so that the next person who touches this patient doesn't start from zero?
  • The patient wants to leave. He is legally competent. He has the right to go. What is your obligation when a patient's autonomy conflicts with their safety?
  • If you had seen this file yesterday, what would you have done differently? What system failed to give you that chance?
After the Simulation — The Debrief

What the room learned by doing.

The debrief is where the simulation becomes training. These four questions guide the conversation from what happened in the room to what will happen differently at the bedside.

What did you prioritize — and why?
There is no single right answer to the order of operations. But there is a right conversation about it. Surface the reasoning, not just the decision. The medication crisis and the contraindication are life-threatening. Did the room go there first?
Where did the system fail M. Rivera?
The insurance lapse was six days old. The supplement was two years old. The 72-hour plan was never started. None of these were discovered at 9am because of a bad nurse. They were discovered because no system existed to catch them earlier.
What would The Signal have changed?
If the daughter had been identified and briefed two weeks ago, she would have known to be there. She might have known about the insurance. She might have known about the St. John's Wort. The Signal is not a luxury. It is a clinical intervention.
What do you do differently on Monday?
Not theoretically. Specifically. One thing. Named. Committed to out loud. The debrief ends when every person in the room has said one concrete thing they will do differently the next time they are preparing a patient for discharge.
"A problem is not a problem until someone owns it.
Until then, it is just a fact waiting to become a crisis."
