Facilitation Philosophy

How This Room Works

Before we begin, you deserve to know exactly what kind of training this is — and what the person at the front of the room has committed to.

Great training doesn't happen by accident.
It's designed.

You are a clinician. You have sat through mandatory trainings that wasted your time, talked down to you, and disappeared from your practice the moment you walked out the door. That is not what this is.

This page is a transparency document. It tells you — before you walk in — how the room will be run, what the trainer has promised to do, and how this program was built on what the evidence shows actually improves transplant patient outcomes. You are here because you care enough to show up. That is what matters most. The trainer's job is to honor that — and to make sure what you learn in this room makes it all the way to the discharge desk.

The Trainer's Promise

Five commitments the trainer makes to every person in the room — before the first slide goes up. These aren't guidelines. They are a contract.

01

I will protect your time.

Every minute of this training exists for a reason. If something isn't serving you, it gets cut. You will never sit in this room wondering why you're here.

02

I will not let one voice take over the room.

Every perspective in this room has value. If a conversation gets dominated — by a peer, by a dynamic, by social pressure — I will redirect it. Not as a punishment. As a protection. Everyone here earned their seat.

03

I will name the uncomfortable things first.

If there is something awkward in the room — a tension, a known problem, an elephant — I will say it out loud so we can work with it instead of around it. Silence doesn't make hard things disappear. It just makes them louder.

04

I will treat your experience as evidence.

You have been at a patient's bedside. You have seen things no textbook describes. When you speak from that experience, it is not anecdote — it is data. This training will be shaped by what you bring into the room.

05

I will know when to get out of the way.

The best moments in any training happen when the trainer stops talking. I will recognize those moments and step back — because the goal was never for you to learn only from me. It was for you to learn from each other, and from yourself.

From the Front of the Room to the Back

This training doesn't stay in one gear. Here is exactly how the trainer's role shifts — and why — as the room comes alive.

📢

Phase One

The Opening Voice

The trainer is at the front. This is the "here's what we know" phase — context, case data, the four gaps, the "why" behind this program. The room needs a foundation before it can build anything.

Delivers the case for change

Establishes shared language

Sets the emotional tone

Opening 20%
🔄

Phase Two

The Facilitator

The trainer moves to the side. Questions replace statements. The room starts doing the thinking. The trainer's job is now to hold the space, keep the energy honest, and make sure every voice gets heard.

Asks instead of tells

Surfaces conflict productively

Redirects, doesn't control

Middle 60%
🎯

Phase Three

The Observer

The trainer steps back. The room owns it now. Participants are applying, deciding, committing. The trainer's job is to observe, affirm, and get out of the way of something real happening.

Observes without interrupting

Affirms ownership

Closes with accountability

Final 20%

"The trainer who cannot stop lecturing has confused their comfort with the learner's need. The room doesn't exist for the trainer. The trainer exists for the room."

— Transplant Discharge Wellness Group, Facilitation Standards
